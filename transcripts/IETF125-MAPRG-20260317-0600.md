[Presentation 6: [Analyzing Compliance and Complications of Integrating Internationalized X.509 Certificates](https://datatracker.ietf.org/meeting/125/materials/slides-125-maprg-analyzing-compliance-and-complications-of-integrating-internationalized-x509-certificates-01)]

**Mingming Zhang:** Hi everyone. I'm Mingming Zhang from Zhongguancun Laboratory. Today I will talk about the multilingual internet. Our recent measurement study published on IMC 2023 sent a wake-up call that is integrating internationalized content in X.509 certificates across compliance gaps and complications that need to resolve.

The Public Key Infrastructure is the security foundation of the internet. It relies on X.509 certificate to bind identities to cryptographic keys. This certificate need to follow some strict standards that define the required formats, structures, and encodings for every single field.

The multilingual internet is driven by ICANN and the Universal Acceptance Initiative, which ensures internet applications and systems can properly handle character beyond the printable ASCII set, like Chinese characters. So when an X.509 certificate contain any of the internationalized content, such as the IDN, IRI, or multilingual text, we call it a Unicert.

So why Unicert break things? Because the broader Unicode space complicates the issuance, parsing, and validation of certificate, which may introduce a potential security or usability issue. Many real-world incidents have shown that improper Unicode handling in certificate can cause cert spoofing, incorrect attribute parsing, or even buffer overflows in client software. However, while X.509 standards support Unicode, the Universal Acceptance readiness of PKI's core mechanism remains largely untested. So this is the gap we aim to fill.

Our study present the first large-scale measurement and empirical security study of Unicert. It's guided by three research questions. The first is: have certificate authorities issued Unicert in compliance with the complex standards? The second is: do mainstream TLS implementations correctly parse Unicert according to normative constraint? The third one is: what are the security and usability impacts of non-compliance issuance and parsing flaws?

So let's start with the Unicert issuance. The first challenge we meet is identifying a definitive standard. How can we establish clear normative requirements for Unicert compliance? The problem is that relevant specifications, such as X.509, DNS, and IDN spec suite, and also CA Browser baseline requirements, are highly interdependent and evolving. There are many updates, revisions, or references here. In addition, many rules for format or encoding of certificate fields are scattered across natural language, the footnotes, or ASN.1 definitions. So a compliance violations may arise from any of these aspect.

To tackle this complexity, we employ RFCGPT, a custom GPT augmented with RFC database to navigate complex requirement and constraint. It helps us identify encoding structure and character constraints for cert fields that allow non-ASCII characters. We have instructed 95 rules covering 36 cert fields with 50 rules missing from existing compliance checks.

This rules are the foundation of our measurement study. We use this rule to build a certificate checking linter and use it to check against over 34 million Unicert collected from the CT database. So let's look at the Unicert issuance ecosystem. These Unicert were mainly came from about 700 organizations, and the issuance numbers are clearly rising, showing that the adoption of Unicert is steadily growing.

From this set, we have identified about 250,000 non-compliant or problematic Unicert, and 65% of them were issued by publicly trusted CAs. These issues mainly fall into three categories. The first is improper character checks, which involves basic flaws such as including the non-printable characters in the printable string field. The second is a lack of value normalization, such as the UTF-8 string not normalized to Unicode NFC form. The last is invalid format or structure, including the formatting errors or invalid encoding methods or any other structural issues that hinder the certificate parsing.

These non-compliant Unicert were issued by over 500 organizations, indicating that the problematic practice is widespread, involving both major global CAs and regional providers. Meanwhile, we also found some requirements may not have been fully covered by the existing linting tool that CAs currently use.

These issues are not just limited to one or two fields. Actually, we have identified that 17 different subject or extension fields that didn't follow a relevant standard or baseline requirement.

There is an interesting case study. We know that DNS names are critical for identifying peer entities. However, we found 27,000 Unicert with malformed IDNs in the DNS name field. Many Punycode IDNs are syntactically allowed under the current CAB baseline requirements; they have valid P-labels and they are resolvable while wildcard DNS. However, when we decode them back to the Unicode, we found there are many special characters in the domain labels that are disallowed by the relevant standard. So there is a conflict. The results reveal a wide range of decoding or character handling anomalies in all of our tested libraries, including the incompatible or over-tolerant decoding, such as using non-standard declared method to decode, or accepting characters beyond standard range.

We uncover some parsing flaws that may enable exploitations. For example, the encoding and decoding mismatches might allow common name forgery, and improper replacement of control characters could enable CRL spoofing. We did find some certificate in CT logs that could be misinterpreted like this, but we have no evidence of a real exploitation yet.

Finally, we also conducted an empirical study in the real-world scenario and uncover some interesting threat services. The first threat is misleading CT monitoring. The second is about user spoofing. We found specially crafted cert fields can manipulate how browser warning pages are displayed. For example, the Chrome's warning page can render bidirectional characters in the malformed common name field. And Firefox page could show misleading information that is derived from a malformed SAN field. This will potentially trick users into trust unverified site.

To sum up, achieving a truly internationalized PKI is currently challenging due to the systematic issue non-compliance and universal parsing flaws. We consider building an internationalized PKI requires collaboration work among CAs, TLS developers, and the relevant standard bodies. We have provided some checking rules, tools, and recommendations to the community to help better handling of Unicert. We hope to bring us closer to a much secure and global PKI. First, let's briefly review the background of NTP. Accurate time plays the vital role in the internet security, such as TLS and RPKI. The Network Time Protocol, or NTP in short, is widely used to synchronize time between different computer systems on the internet. While NTP serves as a critical internet infrastructure, it was not initially designed with security considerations, making it suffer from various attacks, such as time-shifting attacks. In such attack, adversaries could manipulate timestamps in the NTP response or control NTP server to send malicious timestamp to clients. Therefore, as one of the essential mitigation strategies against the time-shifting attacks, the Network Time Security, or NTS in short, has been proposed to protect the authenticity and integrity of NTP packet. In NTS, an server sends response using server-to-client key, and a client uses the key to validate the response. However, NTS cannot judge if the NTP server itself is providing inaccurate time. An inaccurate time sources to propagate errors time information to clients.

To evaluate the degree of timing inaccuracy within the NTP ecosystem, we first collected open NTP servers on the internet. From April 2023 to August 2024, we conducted 66 rounds of scans at weekly intervals. We find around 7 million open NTP servers, 3,000 NTP pool servers, and 21,000 REFID servers. The REFID server is indicated by the REFID field in the NTP response to provide time to clients. We also collected open NTS servers on the internet. From December 2023 to August 2024, we conducted nine rounds of scans at monthly intervals. We find 500 and 83 NTS servers, eight times more than the NTS servers on the public list.

Based on the measurement data, we first analyze the characteristics of NTP server. We find 1,115,000 open NTP servers, seven NTP pool servers, and 1,000 REFID servers are bad timekeepers. That is, their times have an offset greater than 10 seconds from UTC. Inwards, while stratum 0 clocks, such as atomic clock and GPS clock, are traditionally to be considered to be highly accurate time, we find 61.2% open NTP servers and 10.8% REFID servers use stratum 0 clocks are bad timekeepers.

We then analyze the configurations of NTS servers. We find even the number of NTS servers is on the rise, 374 NTS servers are misconfigured, hindering their ability to provide time services. For example, 24 NTS servers use expired or self-signed TLS certificates. 335 NTS servers are associated with NTP servers that are unable to provide correct time and so on.

We then analyze the causes of bad timekeeping. We find 92.1% open NTP servers suffer from synchronization anomalies, such as packet loss, while 6.7% open NTP servers use trustworthy time sources, such as they use inaccurate time servers.

To better understand how NTP server configurations affect the time accuracy, we launched a normalization survey to 377 NTP operators and received 40 email replies. We find they also experienced the interference point time sources and unexpected packet loss. For example, four operators providing inaccurate time use low-cost GPS receivers. Three operators experienced the packet loss due to the improper firewall configurations and ISP security risks.

Based on the measurement results, we uncover two security risks stemming from flawed NTP server configurations. The first is single source vulnerability. As shown in fig, an attacker first manipulate time of an upstream server, by on-path or off-path attacks, such as hijacking the BGP route to the upstream server and manipulate timestamps in the NTP response to the upstream server. After that, when downstream servers query the upstream server, they will receive and adopt the manipulated time, since they use the upstream server as their only time source. For evaluation, we find 2 million and 24,000 NTP servers are configured with only a single upstream time server. Inwards, the most upstream servers having as many as 41,000 downstream servers.

The second vulnerability is dangling IP address. As shown in the fig, an attacker first control an IP address of an upstream server by searching for unavailable time sources. For example, an IP address provided by the cloud cloud service providers are susceptible to be take over due to the nature of dynamic IP leasing clouds. After that, when downstream server queries the upstream server, it will return a malformed packet with malicious timestamps. If downstream server accept the packet, the time will be manipulated. For conservative evaluation, we find 6,000 NTP servers statically configure the IP address of the 37 dangling upstream NTP server, which are deployed in the clouds. For aggressive evaluation, we find 35,000 NTP servers are deployed in clouds and 244 are now dangling.

To mitigate the NTP server vulnerabilities, we have proposed some suggestions. Briefly speaking, we recommend NTP server operators use reputable and diverse time sources, ensure network connectivity, and update in time. Besides, using NTP pool as a time source is also a mitigation, since it periodically monitors time synchronization services, assigns NTP servers based on country. However, NTP pool has two has some limitations. The first is it doesn't support NTS, which makes NTP clients easily vulnerable to man-in-the-middle attacks. The second is its country-first NTP server assignment. The number of NTP servers varies by country, with some having as few as four. This can lead to NTP server centralization, introducing potential security risks. The third is the fixed weights cause overloaded circles. Operator side weights are never adjusted by the NTP pool, and low-weighted servers get overloaded, evicted, recover, and rejoin repeatedly, causing service instability.

To solve the problems of NTP pool, we proposed NTSMon, which use publisher and subscriber model to support the NTS. To be specific, NTSMon periodically monitor for the public NTP and NTS servers, score them based on the time accuracy and availability, and publish a ranked list. We develop a NTSMon client plugin for the widely used NTP client software, such as NTPD and chrony. It auto-fetches the NTP server list and updates NTP client configurations. Besides, NTSMon use adaptive load adjustment, which can reduce the unresponsiveness of NTP servers. In our simulation with true NTP client query traffic, NTSMon can deliver accurate timing services even with fewer time sources than the country-based method, and reduce the service unresponsiveness by 11 times under heavy query loads.

Characterize time accuracy in today's NTP ecosystem, reveal that time inaccuracy is mainly caused by synchronization anomalies and untrustworthy time sources. Identify two security risks stemming from configurations of time sources, including single source vulnerability and dangling IP address vulnerability. Proposing NTSMon to provide clients with an accurate and available NTP and NTS server list. For more details, please read our paper, which was published at IMC 2023. That's all for my presentation.
